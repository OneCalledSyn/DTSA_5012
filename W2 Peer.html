1. (c) Contrasts¶

Let's take a look at the use of contrasts. Recall that a contrast takes the form

$$\sum_{i=1}^t c_i\mu_i = 0,$$

where $\mathbf{c} = (c_1,...,c_t)$ is a constant vector and $\mathbf{\mu} = (\mu_1,...,\mu_t)$ is a parameter vector (e.g., $\mu_1$ is the mean of the $i^{th}$ group).

We can note that $\mathbf{c} = (1,-1,0,0)$ corresponds to the null hypothesis $H_0: \mu_2 - \mu_1 = 0$, where $\mu_1$ is the mean associated with tip1 and $\mu_2$ is the mean associated with tip2. The code below tests this hypothesis.

Repeat this test for the hypothesis $H_0: \mu_4 - \mu_3 = 0$. Interpret the results. What are your conclusions?

Problem 2: Ethics in my Math Class!¶

In your own words, answer the following questions:

  • What is power, in the statistical context?
  • Why is power important?
  • What are potential consequences of ignoring/not including power calculations in statistical analyses?

Statistical power is the probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis given that the alternative hypothesis is true. The higher the power of a test, the less likely it is for a Type II error to occur (accepting the null hypothesis when it is false). Power allows researchers to be able to know how effective their experiment or study is at actually being able to detect "effects of substantive interest". Sufficiently powered studies are more likely to ber reproducible; underpowered studies can suffer from ambiguity in their findings because they are unable to be consistently replicated in the future. For example, if a study has a power of 0.15 and correctly rejects the null hypothesis and reports significant results, there are good odds that the next few replications will fail to correctly discern a significant effect occurring (since 85% of the time it will accept the false null hypothesis). Doing power analysis is a great way to determine how many samples are required to meet a particular power requirement (default suggested power is often 0.80), or to determine power ahead of time if you already know how many samples you will have. It is important to calculate power before gathering the data, as post hoc power analysis will lead to flawed values that can overestimate the true power of the study.

Problem 3: Post-Hoc Tests¶

There's so many different post-hoc tests! Let's try to understand them better. Answer the following questsions in the markdown cell:

  • Why are there multiple post-hoc tests?
  • When would we choose to use Tukey's Method over the Bonferroni correction, and vice versa?
  • Do some outside research on other post-hoc tests. Explain what the method is and when it would be used.

There are multiple post-hoc tests because there are different objectives that researchers might want to achieve and specific concerns they might want to address. Maybe they need a post-hoc test that is on the conservative side, or maybe one that is on the more powerful side. Some tests are built to deal with conditions such as groups with uneven sample sizes or groups that lack variance homogeneity. Different post-hoc tests are built upon different distributions as well, for example Scheffe's method implements an F-distribution while Tukey's HSD uses SRD (Studentized range distribution).

When running multiple statistical tests, Bonferroni's Correction alters the individual alpha level for each test to ensure a predetermined familywise significance level is maintained. Another post-hoc test called Tukey's HSD (Honest Significant Difference) is used to figure out which pairwise groups in the sample are different from each other and which are the same; Tukey's HSD also corrects for the family-wise significant level like Bonferroni's method. Tukey's HSD would be a good choice to examine all of the pairwise groups, whereas Bonferroni allows the option of picking and choosing which pairs to examine. TukeyHSD tends to outperform Bonferroni on a higher number of means, so Bonferroni is a good choice for examining small number of means. Bonferroni correction is also more conservative than TukeyHSD, so much statistical power is required from the post-hoc test can also be a deciding factor in which of the two tests to facilitate.

Scheffe's Method is a post-hoc test that is strong at comparing "all possible simple and complex pairs of means" and is one of the recommended choices for studies that have unequal sample sizes amongst the groups. Another use case where Scheffe's method can outperform the TukeyHSD would be when many contrasts are potentially of interest and a large number of comparisons of not necessarily a fixed size will be made. The resulting confidence intervals will be narrower and thus more precise using Scheffe's method rather than TukeyHSD.
